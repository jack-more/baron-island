# Evidence First Notes

This file is the research basis for the player-world formatter. Each player world should start with evidence, then move into creative simulation.

## Reference Product Evidence: Messenger

Source: Messenger by Abeto  
URL: https://messenger.abeto.co/

Evidence:
- The public landing copy frames the product as a small planet where someone has to make deliveries.
- The live asset bundle includes delivery quest objects, NPC dialogue, checklist UI, outfit controls, hair and shoe variants, emoji controls, quest completion audio, and multiple ambience loops.

Use:
- Make each player world a compact memory planet with simple delivery missions.
- Keep UI minimal and world-native.
- Treat outfits as a core emotional layer, not as decoration.

Source: Awwwards Messenger case study  
URL: https://www.awwwards.com/messenger.html

Evidence:
- The team describes the world as a tiny sphere where the user can walk in any direction and return to the starting point.
- The technical pillars include dynamic camera, upright orientation on curved terrain, and central gravity.
- The planet has seven distinctive areas: neighborhood, plaza, cemetery, beach, mountain temple, forest, and factory.
- The world was hand-modeled, uses a tiny color atlas, and was optimized to feel rich even on an older phone.
- Character clothes and hair are randomized, and updates are streamed smoothly to avoid stutter.
- Multiplayer rooms are capped around 10 users to preserve calm.

Use:
- Baron world should also use seven zones.
- The formatter should always ask for six to eight eras, with one mission per era.
- Social presence should stay small, expressive, and low-friction.

Source: Communication Arts interview with Vicente Lucendo  
URL: https://www.commarts.com/webpicks/messenger

Evidence:
- Messenger was designed to create a feeling between calm and strangeness.
- The small planet layout was chosen so players avoid invisible barriers and can circle the entire world.
- Outlines, color choices, hand-drawn graphics, and detail optimization were central to the art style.
- The team says the story came late, and a more structured story process could have produced a deeper narrative.

Use:
- The formatter solves the story-late problem by forcing the story spine before production.
- Each zone should include a feeling, a mission, a memory object, and a story unlock prompt.

Source: 80 Level article  
URL: https://80.lv/articles/deliver-mail-on-tiny-colorful-planet-in-this-relaxing-web-game

Evidence:
- Messenger is described as a free web-based game made with WebGL and Three.js.
- Users can change clothes and hair and send 3D emoji.

Use:
- Build the prototype as browser-first WebGL or Three.js.
- Make clothes, hair, and emoji a reusable part of the player-world grammar.

Source: Aftermath article  
URL: https://aftermath.site/messenger-browser-game-abeto/

Evidence:
- The world is described as small enough to traverse in minutes, with visible curvature and lived-in detail.
- Other players can appear, communicate through emoji, and follow each other.

Use:
- Player worlds should be small, dense, and quickly learnable.
- Multiplayer should create presence and guidance without open chat.

## Product Laws For The Formatter

- Evidence before imagination.
- Six to eight zones per player.
- One emotional thesis per player.
- One simple mission per zone.
- One story object per mission.
- One interview prompt per zone.
- One outfit unlock per era.
- One rights-light prototype path and one licensed upgrade path.
- Small multiplayer rooms.
- No open text chat in version one.
- Browser-first, smooth, and playable in the first minute.

## Confidence Notes

- Exact NBA, team, UCLA, Crossroads, music, archive, and jersey usage requires licensing review.
